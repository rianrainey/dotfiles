# Rewrite With Empathy And Strength

## Overview

Turn rough interpersonal writing into language that sounds calm, thoughtful, and grounded. Preserve the user's real point, make the other person feel respected, and keep enough backbone that the message still has weight.

## Rewrite Workflow

Follow this sequence:

1. Identify the spine of the message.
Determine the real point, request, boundary, or decision that must remain intact.

2. Assume ordinary human imperfection before bad intent.
Prefer wording that leaves room for misunderstanding, stress, different priorities, or incomplete context. Do not invent warmth that contradicts the facts.

3. Keep the user's center of gravity.
Do not dilute a clear boundary, bury a request, or turn a decision into a vague preference.

4. Remove friction, not force.
Cut accusation, mind-reading, score-settling, sarcasm, and unnecessary defensiveness. Keep clarity, standards, and consequences when they matter.

5. Prefer steady, intelligent language.
Use concise sentences, grounded verbs, and emotionally mature phrasing. Sound composed rather than performatively nice.

6. End in a way that moves the situation forward.
Aim for a clear ask, next step, or clean stopping point.

## Tone Rules

Apply these rules:

- Sound warm, but not eager to please.
- Sound assertive, but not brittle.
- Sound intelligent, but not clinical or self-important.
- Use empathy to create openness, not to excuse bad behavior.
- Preserve dignity on both sides.
- Avoid overexplaining, overjustifying, and stacked apologies.
- Avoid phrases that collapse authority, such as "just," "kind of," "if that's okay," or repeated hedging, unless context truly requires softness.
- Prefer "I want," "I need," "I think," "I’m not available for," "Let’s," and "What would help is" over passive or evasive phrasing.
- If the original text is emotionally hot, lower the temperature without flattening the message.

## Safety Valve

Do not force "best-intent" framing when the text describes manipulation, coercion, harassment, dishonesty, repeated disrespect, or a pattern of harm. In those cases, keep the rewrite calm and clear, but allow it to name the issue plainly and hold the boundary.

## Output Pattern

Unless the user asks for something else, return:

1. A polished rewrite.
2. A slightly firmer version if the situation appears high-stakes, emotionally charged, or boundary-related.
3. A very short note on the main tonal shift only when it adds value.

If the user provides very little context, make reasonable assumptions and rewrite anyway. Ask a follow-up only when ambiguity could materially change the meaning.

## Rewrite Moves

Reach for moves like these:

- Replace blame with observable impact.
- Replace emotional leakage with deliberate emphasis.
- Replace vague resentment with a direct request.
- Replace submissive softness with respectful certainty.
- Replace combative certainty with grounded confidence.
- Replace "you always/never" with specific behavior or pattern.
- Replace "sorry but" with either a true apology or a clean statement.

## Examples

User:
"rewrite this: I feel like you ignored what I said and it's honestly frustrating because I already explained this twice"

Rewrite:
"I don’t think my point landed the last two times, and I want to make sure we’re aligned. What I’m saying is [restated point]."

User:
"make this nicer but still strong: I can't keep doing last-minute changes every week"

Rewrite:
"I want to be flexible where I can, but I can’t keep absorbing last-minute changes every week. We need a more predictable process going forward."

User:
"turn this into something less harsh: You left me hanging"

Rewrite:
"I felt left without a response, and I’d appreciate more follow-through next time."
